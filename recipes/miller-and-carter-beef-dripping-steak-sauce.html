Miller & Carter-Style Beef Dripping Steak Sauce

A rich, glossy and intensely beefy steak sauce made with beef dripping, browned beef, shallots, red wine, port and concentrated stock. This is an unofficial home copycat rather than the restaurant's published recipe.

Makes
4 small sauce pots
Prep time
10 mins
Cook time
35–45 mins
Best served with
Steak and chips
Cooking safety: hot beef dripping can spit. Add the wine and port away from an open flame and keep your face clear of the steam.

Ingredients

  • 50 g beef dripping
  • 100 g fatty stewing beef, such as chuck, skirt or brisket, thinly sliced
  • 2 shallots, finely diced
  • 3 garlic cloves, finely chopped
  • ½ star anise
  • 80 ml dry red wine
  • 20 ml ruby port
  • 500 ml strong beef stock, preferably gelatin-rich
  • ½ teaspoon brown or white sugar
  • A few drops of red-wine vinegar, to taste
  • Fine salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
  • ¼ teaspoon xanthan gum
  • Alternative thickener: 1 teaspoon cornflour mixed with 1 tablespoon cold water
  • Optional: resting juices from the cooked steaks

Method

  1. Heat a wide saucepan over medium-high heat. Add roughly one-third of the beef dripping.
  2. Add the sliced beef, shallots, garlic and star anise. Fry for 5–6 minutes, stirring regularly, until the beef and shallots are deeply browned.
  3. Remove and discard the star anise. Pour in the red wine and port, then scrape the browned flavour from the bottom of the pan.
  4. Boil briskly until the wine mixture has reduced to roughly one-third of its original volume.
  5. Add the beef stock and the remaining dripping. Bring to a boil, then simmer uncovered for 20–30 minutes, until reduced by about half and intensely beefy.
  6. Strain through a fine sieve into a clean saucepan. Press the solids gently to release the liquid, but do not force them through the sieve.
  7. Add the sugar and black pepper. Add salt only if needed, because concentrated stock can already be salty. Add the red-wine vinegar two or three drops at a time until the sauce tastes rich but not greasy.
  8. Place over low heat and gradually whisk in the xanthan gum. Allow a minute for it to thicken before adding more. If using cornflour instead, whisk in the slurry and simmer for 1–2 minutes.
  9. Stir in any resting juices from the steaks, taste once more, and serve hot.

Tips

  • Use a strong, gelatin-rich beef stock rather than a weak stock cube for the best glossy texture and depth.
  • The finished sauce should be pourable like a rich jus, not as thick as conventional gravy.
  • Deep browning in the first step is essential; pale beef and shallots will produce a flatter-tasting sauce.
  • Add vinegar very cautiously. It should lift the beef flavour without making the sauce taste sour.
  • Refrigerate in a sealed container for up to 3 days, or freeze in individual portions. Reheat gently and whisk before serving.
